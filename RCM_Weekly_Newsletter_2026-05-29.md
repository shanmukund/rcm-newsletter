## Section 01 — Regulatory / Payment Updates {#section-01}

### CMS Proposes $775 Billion Medicaid Shake-Up: State-Directed Payments Capped at Medicare Rates — What Practices Billing Medicaid Must Know Now

On May 20, 2026, CMS published a landmark proposed rule (CMS-2449-P) that would fundamentally restructure how states fund Medicaid supplemental payments — and how much physicians in Medicaid-heavy practices can expect to receive. The proposed rule targets **state-directed payments (SDPs)** and certain targeted **fee-for-service supplemental payments**, proposing to cap them at Medicare rate equivalents and phase out entire payment categories over time.

**What Is Being Proposed:**

The rule proposes to extend limits on total payment rates for **all state-directed payments** across service categories, tying payment levels to Medicare rates. Specifically:
- Cap state-directed payments at **100% of Medicare rates** in Medicaid expansion states
- Cap at **110% of Medicare rates** in non-expansion states
- Apply caps for rating periods beginning on or after **July 4, 2025** (retroactive pressure)
- Phase out "uniform increase" payment arrangements entirely over time

The Congressional Budget Office estimates the rule would generate **$775 billion in total savings over 10 years**, including **$510 billion in federal savings**. For states, this means a dramatic reduction in their ability to use directed payments as a supplemental funding mechanism — and for provider practices, it signals downward pressure on Medicaid reimbursement in states that have historically topped up payments above Medicare baselines.

**Why This Matters for Independent Practices:**

Many independent physician groups — particularly in primary care, behavioral health, FQHC-adjacent specialties, and rural medicine — derive meaningful revenue from Medicaid managed care plans whose supplemental rates are currently higher than Medicare equivalents. If finalized as proposed:
- Practices in expansion states billing MCOs that currently receive above-Medicare Medicaid rates will face reimbursement reductions
- The phase-out of uniform increase arrangements removes a funding layer that has historically propped up primary care reimbursement in many states
- Rural hospitals and safety-net practices are disproportionately affected

**Concurrent Developments:**

Separately, the CMS CY 2026 Physician Fee Schedule maintains the two-tier APM conversion factor structure — qualifying APM participants receive a **3.77% conversion factor** versus **3.26% for non-APM participants**. With the across-the-board **-2.5% efficiency adjustment** also in effect, non-APM practices face a compounding Medicare reimbursement headwind entering the second half of 2026.

**Comment Period:** Comments on CMS-2449-P are due by **July 21, 2026**.

> **Key Insight:** If your practice has a Medicaid payer mix above 20%, pull your state's current Medicaid managed care contract rates and compare them to the Medicare equivalent. Identify which service lines rely on state-directed payment supplements. The rule is proposed — not final — but the comment deadline is July 21, and the financial modeling is worth doing now regardless of the outcome.

## Section 02 — Prior Authorization / Payer Relations {#section-02}

### UnitedHealthcare Eliminates Prior Authorization for 30% of Services Affecting 50 Million Members — Echocardiograms, Outpatient Surgery, and Therapies Among First to Go

In the most significant single-payer prior authorization reduction in U.S. healthcare history, UnitedHealthcare announced on **May 5, 2026** that it will eliminate prior authorization requirements for approximately **30% of services** that currently require advance approval — with full implementation by year-end 2026. The announcement affects approximately **50 million members** across commercial, Medicare Advantage, and employer-sponsored plans.

**What Is Being Eliminated:**

UHC identified several high-friction categories as the first wave of PA eliminations:
- Select **outpatient surgeries** (full list to be published on UHCProvider.com before changes take effect)
- **Diagnostic tests** including echocardiograms and select imaging studies
- Certain **outpatient therapies** — physical therapy, occupational therapy, and speech-language pathology
- **Chiropractic care** services

This builds on UHC's April 20, 2026 announcement of accelerated payment support for rural healthcare providers and PA exemptions for rural care providers — a move designed to improve financial stability for rural hospitals and reduce care team administrative burden.

**CMS Early Adopter Initiative (May 13, 2026):**

On May 13, CMS launched a new **Early Adopter Initiative** to work with a coalition of stakeholders to facilitate **electronic prior authorization (ePA) adoption** ahead of the 2027 regulatory deadline (CMS-0057-F). The initiative accelerates implementation of HL7 FHIR-based ePA APIs across Medicare Advantage, Medicaid MCOs, CHIP, and Marketplace plans. The key 2026 benchmark already in effect: impacted payers must issue PA decisions within **72 hours for standard requests** and **24 hours for expedited requests** (as of January 1, 2026).

**Industry-Wide PA Reduction Scoreboard:**

| Payer | 2026 PA Action | Members Impacted | Timeline |
|---|---|---|---|
| UnitedHealthcare | Eliminate PA for 30% of services | ~50 million | By year-end 2026 |

**What Stays:** UHC is not eliminating PA for high-cost specialty biologics, complex inpatient procedures, or services with documented overutilization patterns. Specialty drug authorization, transplants, and advanced imaging with high denial rates remain subject to review.

> **Action Required:** Log into UHCProvider.com and download the updated PA requirement list as each service category publishes. Mark the elimination dates in your scheduling and authorization team's calendar. Practices with high echocardiogram or outpatient therapy volumes can expect to recover 20–35 minutes per avoided PA request — and should update intake workflows accordingly before eliminations take effect.

## Section 04 — Coding Updates {#section-04}

### CPT 2026 AI-Assisted Diagnostic Codes Now Live: New Billable Codes for Lung Nodule Detection, Stroke AI, and Mammogram Comparison — Are You Capturing Them?

CPT 2026 introduced several coding advances that are directly billable today — yet most practices have not updated their superbills or chargemasters to capture them. Five months into the CPT 2026 cycle, revenue from new AI-assisted diagnostic codes remains largely uncaptured at independent practices.

**New Radiology AI Category I Codes (CPT 2026):**

The AMA CPT 2026 code set includes new **Category I codes** for AI tools performing specific diagnostic functions:
- **Lung nodule detection on chest CT** — AI analysis of CT scan for pulmonary nodule identification
- **Stroke sign detection on brain MRI/CT** — AI-assisted identification of acute stroke indicators
- **Automated comparison of prior mammograms** — AI-driven comparison analysis supporting radiologist review

These codes require physician attestation that the AI tool was used and that the physician reviewed the AI output as part of the clinical decision. Practices that have integrated AI diagnostic tools but have not billed the corresponding CPT codes are leaving newly established revenue on the table.

**Telehealth Code Restructuring — CPT 98016 Replaces G2012:**

A new telemedicine subsection added **17 codes** for synchronous evaluation and management across new and established patients. Key transition:
- **CPT 98016** replaces **HCPCS G2012** for brief virtual check-ins — practices still billing G2012 should update billing systems immediately
- The new telemedicine subcategory creates cleaner documentation pathways for audio-only and video-assisted encounters

**AMA Appendix S — AI Classification Framework:**

CPT 2026 formally defined three categories of AI in clinical use through **AMA Appendix S**:

| AI Category | Definition | Billing Implication |
|---|---|---|
| Assistive AI | Provides alerts/suggestions; clinician retains full decision authority | No separate billable code; embedded in E/M |
| Augmentative AI | Performs analysis that augments but does not replace clinical judgment | New Category III codes in imaging & physiology |
| Autonomous AI | Executes clinical task independently; human review of output only | Category I codes where approved; attestation required |

New **Category III additions** describe augmentative data analysis in imaging and physiologic measurements — these are temporary tracking codes that transition to Category I once utilization data supports permanent assignment.

**Behavioral Health Coding — Protected From Efficiency Cut:**

CMS specifically **exempted behavioral health and psychiatry services** from the -2.5% RVU efficiency adjustment in CY 2026, including:
- Psychotherapy codes (individual, group, crisis)
- Psychiatric evaluation and management
- Behavioral health integration (BHI) codes
- Remote mental health service G-codes

Digital mental health bundles and behavioral health integration codes received **stronger payment structures** in the 2026 fee schedule — a deliberate CMS signal to encourage mental health access expansion.

> **Key Insight:** If your practice employs AI diagnostic tools — even as a licensed module within your EHR or PACS — review whether those tools qualify for the new CPT 2026 Category I billing codes. The attestation requirement is straightforward: document that the AI was used, identify the tool, and note that you reviewed its output. Missed billing on AI-assisted reads is pure revenue leakage from a capability you are already paying for.

## Section 07 — Compliance Corner {#section-07}

### CMS Price Transparency Enforcement Now Active: Machine-Readable Files Must Include Median Allowed Amounts, Type 2 NPIs, and CEO Attestation

CMS formalized a new round of hospital price transparency requirements in the **CY 2026 OPPS/ASC Final Rule** (November 2025), with a grace period for implementation that officially ended **January 1, 2026** — and enforcement that became active **April 1, 2026**. Practices operating as hospital outpatient departments or ASC-affiliated entities need to verify compliance with the updated machine-readable file (MRF) standards.

**What Changed in the 2026 Price Transparency Update:**

Three specific additions to the hospital MRF requirements are now enforceable:

1. **Median Allowed Amounts Required** — Hospitals must replace the estimated allowed amount with the **median allowed amount** and add the **10th and 90th percentile allowed amounts**. This shifts price transparency from theoretical charges to actual negotiated amounts — giving patients (and competitors) more accurate cost comparison data.

2. **Type 2 NPI Required** — Hospitals must include **Type 2 NPIs** specifically for codes 27 (hospital unit) and 28 (hospital) in their MRFs. This standardizes price data across hospital systems and enables CMS to cross-reference MRFs against health plan transparency files.

3. **CEO Attestation Required** — Hospital MRFs must now include a formal attestation that all standard charge information is "true, accurate, and complete," along with the **name of the CEO, President, or designated senior official**. This creates explicit executive accountability for MRF accuracy.

**Health Plan Transparency in Coverage (TiC) Updates:**

Separately, CMS proposed updates to the **Transparency in Coverage rules** for health plans (December 2025), which would make health plan and TPA pricing data more standardized and accessible. While still in the comment/finalization process, practices should be aware that payer MRF accuracy will improve — making underpayment detection easier for practices with the right analytics tools.

**Ongoing HIPAA Enforcement — Tracking Technologies:**

The Office for Civil Rights (OCR) continues its 2025–2026 enforcement campaign targeting **third-party tracking pixels** embedded in practice websites and patient portals. Practices that have not yet audited their patient-facing digital properties for HIPAA-violating trackers remain exposed. OCR has issued penalties ranging from **$50,000 to $1.9 million** in 2025 enforcement actions related to tracking technology violations.

> **Action Required:** If your practice operates under a hospital outpatient department billing arrangement or is affiliated with an ASC, confirm your MRF was updated by April 1, 2026 with median allowed amounts, Type 2 NPIs, and the CEO attestation statement. For independent practices, audit your patient portal and website for third-party tracking scripts — free tools like Blacklight (themarkup.org) scan public-facing pages without technical expertise required.

## Section 10 — This Week's Action Items {#section-10}

### Action Checklist — Week of May 29, 2026

**1. File Comments on CMS-2449-P if You Bill Medicaid**
Comment deadline: July 21, 2026. Calculate your Medicaid payer mix and compare current managed care rates to Medicare equivalents. If your practice would be materially affected by the proposed payment cap, submit comments through the Federal Register or through your state medical association.

**2. Download UHC's Updated PA Elimination List**
Log into UHCProvider.com and retrieve the updated prior authorization requirement list for your specialty. If you bill echocardiograms, outpatient therapy, or select outpatient surgery, identify the elimination timeline and update your scheduling team's authorization workflow accordingly.

**3. Audit CPT 2026 AI-Assisted Diagnostic Code Capture**
If your practice uses any AI diagnostic tool (even as a module within your PACS, EHR, or imaging system), verify whether it qualifies for the new CPT 2026 Category I codes. Check your chargemaster and superbill for these codes and establish the physician attestation documentation standard.

**4. Update Virtual Visit Billing from G2012 to CPT 98016**
If your practice bills brief virtual check-ins, confirm your billing system has transitioned from HCPCS G2012 to CPT 98016. Continued use of G2012 for new visits will generate rejections.

**5. Calculate Your Denial Resubmission Rate**
Pull 90 days of denial data and calculate: (denied claims resubmitted ÷ total denied claims). If this number is below 70%, identify the top 3 denial codes by volume and assign specific resubmission ownership and deadlines. The 60% industry never-resubmission rate is a solvable problem — and recovering these claims requires discipline, not new technology.

**6. Verify Price Transparency MRF Compliance (Hospital-Affiliated Practices)**
If your practice bills under a hospital or ASC facility agreement, confirm the machine-readable file was updated by April 1, 2026 with median allowed amounts, Type 2 NPIs, and CEO attestation. Enforcement is now active.

**7. Audit Third-Party Tracking Pixels on Patient-Facing Properties**
Run a Blacklight scan (themarkup.org) on your practice website and patient portal login page. If tracking pixels are present touching scheduling or login flows without a signed BAA, remove them or obtain the BAA this week.

**8. Ask Your RCM Vendor One Question**
Evaluate your current RCM vendor's autonomous AI roadmap: "What is your plan to deploy agentic AI for practices under 20 physicians?" If they cannot articulate a specific timeline, it is worth evaluating whether your current platform will remain competitive as the market consolidates around autonomous RCM solutions.

> **Action Required:** The denial resubmission gap and the CPT 2026 AI code capture gap are this week's highest-ROI recovery opportunities — both recoverable with zero additional technology spend. Start there.
