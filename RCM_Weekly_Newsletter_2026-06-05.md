## Section 01 — Regulatory / Payment Updates {#section-01}

### CY 2027 Medicare Advantage & Part D Rule Effective June 1: PA Continuity Now Mandatory, Star Ratings Overhaul Drops 11 Admin Measures, and Medicaid Work Requirements Begin

Two landmark CMS rules took effect June 1, 2026, reshaping how practices interact with Medicare Advantage payers and their Medicaid patient populations.

**CY 2027 Medicare Advantage & Part D Final Rule (Effective June 1, 2026)**

CMS's finalized CY 2027 MA and Part D rule (published April 6, 2026) governs coverage beginning January 1, 2027, but its marketing, enrollment, and administrative provisions began taking effect June 1 and October 1, 2026. Key changes impacting practices:

**Star Ratings Overhaul:** CMS removed 11 administrative process measures from the Star Ratings program where beneficiaries cannot meaningfully differentiate between high-performing plans. In their place, CMS added a new Part C Depression Screening and Follow-Up measure starting with the 2027 measurement year — signaling continued CMS emphasis on behavioral health integration. The change reduces plan administrative overhead but shifts pressure to practices to close behavioral health documentation gaps for MA-affiliated patients.

**48-Hour SOA Waiting Period Eliminated:** The fixed 48-hour Scope of Appointment waiting period is gone as of October 1, 2026. Same-day Medicare appointments are allowed again — though the SOA must still be completed before the personal marketing appointment starts. Marketing and sales call retention drops to six years (three years must be audio).

**Part D Redesign Codification:** The rule codifies the IRA of 2022 Part D redesign: elimination of the coverage gap, reduced annual out-of-pocket threshold, zero cost sharing in the catastrophic phase, and the Manufacturer Discount Program (10% on initial coverage phase drugs, 20% in catastrophic phase). Critical for practices prescribing high-cost specialty drugs — patient cost exposure under Part D has materially changed.

**Medicaid Community Engagement Interim Final Rule (CMS-2454-IFC, June 1, 2026)**

On June 1, 2026, CMS simultaneously issued an interim final rule implementing a new statutory Medicaid work requirement. Non-pregnant adults ages 19–64 who are not entitled to or enrolled in Medicare must now demonstrate **80 hours per month** of qualifying community engagement activities — employment, work program participation, community service, or enrollment in education at least half-time — or earn at least $580/month (80x the federal minimum wage) as a condition of Medicaid eligibility.

States must implement by January 1, 2027; the regulations are effective July 31, 2026. Exemptions exist for pregnant and postpartum women, tribal members, veterans with total disability ratings, medically frail individuals, caregivers, and those already satisfying SNAP or TANF work requirements.

**Why This Matters for Billing:** Practices with Medicaid-dependent patient panels — particularly safety-net clinics, primary care, behavioral health, and rural medicine — face potential enrollment attrition starting in 2027. Revenue cycle teams should model coverage disruption scenarios now.

> **Key Insight:** The June 1 dual rule issuance is the clearest signal yet that federal payer policy is tightening simultaneously on Medicare Advantage (administrative simplification) and Medicaid (eligibility restriction). Practices in the middle — billing both programs — should stress-test their payer mix assumptions for 2027.

## Section 02 — Prior Authorization / Payer Relations {#section-02}

### CMS Extends ePA Requirements to Drug Authorizations in New Proposed Rule — Plus the 90-Day PA Continuity Guarantee Now in Effect and the UHC–NYP Contract Collapse

**CMS Drug PA Interoperability Proposed Rule (April 10, 2026)**

On April 10, 2026, CMS issued a proposed rule extending federal interoperability and prior authorization requirements to cover drugs for the first time. The rule — Medicare and Medicaid Programs; Interoperability Standards and Prior Authorization for Drugs — would require Medicare Advantage organizations, Medicaid managed care plans, CHIP agencies, and QHP issuers on FFEs to:

- Support electronic prior authorization (ePA) for drug requests via HL7 FHIR APIs
- Issue PA decisions within shorter standardized timeframes for drug requests
- Increase transparency on drug PA metrics (approval rates, denial rates, turnaround times)

If finalized, most provisions would take effect October 1, 2027. For practices with heavy specialty drug utilization — oncology, rheumatology, neurology — this proposed rule signals future relief from the manual drug PA burden that currently adds 16+ hours per week per practice in administrative work.

**90-Day PA Continuity Now in Effect**

As of January 1, 2026, when a patient changes insurance companies mid-treatment, the new plan must honor existing prior authorizations for benefit-equivalent in-network services during a 90-day transition period. This provision — part of the broader industry PA simplification commitment — directly protects practices from mid-course treatment disruptions and associated rebilling.

**Industry PA Scoreboard: 50 Insurers, 257 Million Members**

Approximately 50 insurers have pledged to simplify and reduce prior authorization requirements across commercial, Medicare Advantage, and managed Medicaid plans covering **257 million Americans**. Participating insurers have collectively eliminated **11% of requirements** since the announcement — representing **6.5 million fewer PA requests** industry-wide. The next milestone: by 2027, the coalition commits to implementing standardized ePA and processing at least 80% of electronic approvals in real time.

**The UHC–NewYork-Presbyterian Network Collapse: A Warning for MA Practices**

Starting June 1, 2026, all NewYork-Presbyterian hospitals and Medical Group practices became out-of-network for most UnitedHealthcare Medicare Advantage members after contract negotiations failed. Patients undergoing active treatment before June 1 are protected as in-network until August 29, 2026, after which non-emergency visits become out-of-network unless UHC approves continuation. The dispute underscores a growing tension: as MA plans cut costs (including administrative burden), provider contract terms are tightening — and patients and practices pay the price when talks break down.

> **Action Required:** If you participate in Medicare Advantage networks and operate near a major health system with ongoing payer contract disputes, alert your front desk now — patients may not know their coverage status has changed. A single out-of-network surprise bill generates an average 4.2 additional staff hours to resolve.

## Section 04 — Coding Updates {#section-04}

### Coding Alert: Collaborative Care Management Codes 99492–99494 Retiring — Claims Will Deny — Plus 2026 NCCI Updates for Neurology, GI, and AI-Assisted Diagnostics

**Critical Transition: Collaborative Care Management Codes**

Practices billing Collaborative Care Management (CoCM) services face an immediate compliance risk: the legacy CPT codes **99492, 99493, and 99494** are being replaced by new G-codes **G0568, G0570**, and a companion code. Practices that continue billing the old CPT codes will receive claim denials. This transition affects primary care practices, behavioral health integration programs, and any practice operating a CoCM model for mental health conditions.

CoCM programs bill for the psychiatric consultant, behavioral health care manager, and treating physician coordination. The code structure itself is preserved — the specific time thresholds and documentation requirements carry forward — but the billing codes must be updated in every EHR template, billing system, and clearinghouse mapping immediately.

**2026 NCCI Policy Manual Updates**

CMS has released the 2026 National Correct Coding Initiative (NCCI) policy manual updates, with material changes in two high-risk specialties:

- **Neurology and Neuromuscular Procedures:** Updated bundling rules and edit pairs for neurophysiologic monitoring, EEG, EMG, and nerve conduction studies. Practices billing both the professional and technical components must verify modifier 26/TC usage against updated NCCI edits.
- **Gastroenterology:** Clarified bundling rules for procedural families including colonoscopy with biopsy, EGD with dilation, and ERCP combinations. GI practices billing multiple procedure codes per session should audit against the updated NCCI tables to identify edit pairs that could trigger automatic denials or downcoding.

**AI-Assisted Digital Diagnostic Codes**

CPT 2026 expanded the billable code set for AI-assisted diagnostics (first introduced as the "AI codes" in late 2025). Practices in radiology, pathology, and cardiology should confirm their AI tools are associated with specific CPT codes and that documentation supports medical necessity for the AI-assisted interpretation — not merely the underlying diagnostic test.

**E/M Split/Shared Visit Clarifications**

Updated guidance for 2026 clarifies split/shared E/M documentation when a visit is split between a physician and an NPP. The "substantive portion" standard (more than half of time, or the history, exam, or MDM component) remains operative, but payer-specific guidance from UHC, Aetna, and Cigna has narrowed acceptable documentation. Audit your templates before year-end.

> **Warning:** The CoCM code transition is not optional — it takes effect now and denials begin immediately on old codes. Pull a report of all CoCM claims billed in the past 30 days and verify the code set in your system. A denial rate spike on behavioral health claims is the most visible symptom of this code gap.

**The Revenue Velocity Equation**

Every percentage point of clean claim rate improvement removes approximately **0.5–1 day** from Days in AR, because rework adds 30–60 days to the average touched claim's collection cycle. A practice moving from 92% to 97% clean claim rate recovers 2.5–5 days from AR — the equivalent of 6–12% faster cash velocity. For a $5M annual revenue practice, each recovered day in AR represents roughly $13,700 in accelerated collections.

**The Prevention-First Model**

The industry is shifting from "deny and appeal" to "prevent and submit clean." The operational levers:

- **Front-end eligibility verification at every touchpoint** — not just scheduling, but also the day-prior check and day-of confirmation for high-payer-mix patients

- **Patient financial responsibility pre-clearance** — with patient collections at 35–40% of total practice revenue, pre-service balance estimation and payment plan setup is no longer optional

## Section 07 — Compliance Corner {#section-07}

### Compliance Deadlines Pile Up: Price Transparency Now Enforced, Medicaid Work Requirement Comments Due July 31, and CMS Drug PA Rule Seeks Input

**Hospital Price Transparency Enforcement — Active Since April 1, 2026**

CMS began enforcing the updated hospital price transparency requirements on April 1, 2026. All hospital machine-readable files (MRFs) must now include:

- **Median allowed amounts** (replacing estimated allowed amounts)
- **10th and 90th percentile allowed amounts** per service
- **Type 2 National Provider Identifiers (NPIs)** — specifically active codes associated with primary tax codes 27 (hospital unit) and 28 (hospital)

The effective date of the new requirements was January 1, 2026, but CMS granted a 90-day enforcement grace period through March 31. As of April 1, full enforcement is active. Importantly: hospitals that violate price transparency requirements can reduce their civil monetary penalty by **35%** by waiving the right to an administrative law judge hearing.

**For Independent Practices:** While hospital-level MRF requirements do not directly apply to physician practices, patient-facing price estimate requirements and good faith estimate obligations under the No Surprises Act remain fully in effect. Any practice billing facility fees through a hospital outpatient department or ASC arrangement must confirm the facility's MRF compliance — because payer auditors are increasingly cross-referencing billing against MRF data.

**Medicaid Community Engagement Rule — Comment Period Open Until July 31, 2026**

The CMS-2454-IFC interim final rule published June 3, 2026, is open for public comment until **July 31, 2026**. Practices with significant Medicaid patient populations — particularly primary care, behavioral health, OB/GYN, and rural medicine — should consider submitting comments through the Federal Register portal. Key comment angles: the transition period for newly non-compliant enrollees, exemption documentation burden, and impact on medically frail patient populations whose eligibility may be disputed.

**CMS Drug PA Interoperability Rule — Comment Deadline**

The proposed rule expanding ePA to drug authorizations (April 14, 2026 Federal Register) carried a standard 60-day comment period, placing the comment deadline at approximately **June 13, 2026**. Practices with heavy specialty drug portfolios (oncology, rheumatology, neurology) should coordinate with their billing vendors on comments supporting the proposed 2027 implementation timeline.

> **Warning:** The price transparency landscape is shifting faster than most practices realize. CMS is cross-referencing MRF data, payer allowed-amount reports, and claim-level billing data to identify systematic discrepancies. If your facility billing arrangement relies on outdated rate assumptions, the mismatch may generate both compliance exposure and payer contract renegotiation pressure.
