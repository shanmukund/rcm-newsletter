## Section 01 — Regulatory/Payment Updates
### CMS PFS Correction, 3.26% Rate Increase, and Part D $2,100 Cap Shape the Q1 Billing Landscape

The Federal Register published a correction to the CY 2026 Physician Fee Schedule final rule on March 12, 2026, clarifying payment policies for Part B services that took effect January 1. The 3.26% aggregate rate increase for all physicians remains in effect, representing the most meaningful positive adjustment in several years. Simultaneously, the Medicare Part D out-of-pocket cap of $2,100 (indexed from the 2025 $2,000 cap) is actively reshaping patient cost-sharing and pharmacy benefit designs — with downstream implications for practices managing chronic disease patients on high-cost medications.

**Key Payment Metrics (CY 2026 PFS)**
- Physician rate increase: +3.26% across all specialties
- Medicare Advantage benchmark rates: +5.06% increase to plans
- Part D annual out-of-pocket cap: $2,100
- Anti-obesity medications: now covered under Medicaid as outpatient drugs

**CMS MA Provider Directory Requirements:** MA plans must now submit updated provider data within 30 days of becoming aware of any change and must attest annually to directory accuracy. Practices should audit their directory listings with every MA payer they contract with — inaccurate listings can trigger clean claim rejections.

**Bottom Line Insight:** The 3.26% PFS rate increase is a rare tailwind, but it is partially offset by conversion factor reductions in prior years. Practices should model the net impact on their top 20 procedure codes by payer mix before declaring a revenue win.

## Section 02 — Prior Authorization / Payer Relations
### AHIP Voluntary PA Reforms Now Live — Health Plans Serving 270 Million Begin Delivering on Commitments

Six months after AHIP, BCBSA, and leading health plans announced multi-year voluntary prior authorization commitments to HHS Secretary Kennedy and CMS Administrator Oz, the first wave of reforms took effect January 1, 2026. The initiative covers health plans serving nearly 270 million Americans and introduces concrete timelines and continuity of care protections.

**Current Live Commitments (Effective January 1, 2026):**
- Individual plans committed to specific, measurable reductions in medical prior authorization requirements
- When a patient changes insurance during an ongoing course of treatment, the new plan must honor existing prior authorizations for benefit-equivalent in-network services for a 90-day transition period
- Payers must provide specific denial reasons for every PA refusal (not just generic "not medically necessary")

**Coming in 2027:**
- At least 80% of electronic PA approvals with complete clinical documentation must be answered in real-time

**MA-Specific Restriction (Active Now):** Medicare Advantage plans are now restricted from reopening previously approved inpatient hospital admissions (unless there is clear error or fraud), a significant protection against mid-stay authorization reversals.

**Payer Network Pullbacks:**
- UnitedHealthcare reduced geographic footprint by 109 fewer counties
- Humana exited 3 states and 194 fewer counties
- Aetna reduced coverage by 1 state and 100 fewer counties
- UNC Health is going out-of-network with Humana, WellCare, and HCSC MA plans in 2026

**Action Required:** The 90-day continuity of care rule for plan-switchers requires practices to document when a patient's existing PA was approved and by which plan. Update intake processes to capture this information so billing staff can invoke continuity protections and avoid requesting redundant authorizations.

The RCM AI landscape has crossed a meaningful threshold in 2026. According to current data, 74% of hospitals are implementing some form of revenue-cycle automation — up sharply from prior years — while the share using AI broadly for RCM tasks sits at 63%, primarily concentrated in lower-risk functions like data analysis and eligibility checks. **Adoption Data Points:**

**Agentic AI: The Next Step After RPA**

Agentic AI systems can handle end-to-end processes — from eligibility check through payment posting — with limited human intervention. **Key Insight:** 2026 is the tipping point where three forces converged: AI matured enough to handle tasks it previously got wrong, claim denial volumes reached a scale that made manual workflows economically unworkable, and staffing shortages made the cost of staying manual impossible to justify.

## Section 04 — Coding Updates
### CPT 2026 Delivers 418 Changes — Coronary Revascularization and Radiation Oncology See Most Significant Restructuring

The AMA's CPT 2026 code set, effective January 1, 2026, delivered the largest set of changes in several years: 288 new codes, 84 deletions, and 46 revisions. Q1 2026 is the critical window for auditing documentation and coding workflows to ensure compliance. AAPC's updated CPC exam and codebooks reflect these changes, and practices should confirm their coding staff and billing software are operating on the 2026 code set.

**418 Total CPT Changes at a Glance:**
- 288 new codes added
- 84 codes deleted
- 46 codes revised
- ICD-10-CM FY 2026 guidelines (effective October 1, 2025) introduced hundreds of new diagnosis codes, particularly in injury-related categories

**Coronary Revascularization — Major Restructure:**
CPT 2026 introduces one of the largest updates to coronary revascularization procedures in the code set's history. New codes and structural changes better reflect modern interventional cardiology procedures. Practices performing cardiac catheterization and coronary intervention should audit their superbills and chargemaster immediately.

**Radiation Oncology — Most Significant Overhaul in a Decade:**
- Legacy delivery codes 77402, 77407, and 77412 (simple, intermediate, complex) have been revised into Level 1, 2, and 3 treatment delivery codes
- IMRT delivery codes 77385 and 77386 have been deleted — IMRT is now consolidated into the three-level treatment delivery structure
- Radiation oncology teams must update documentation protocols to justify level-based billing

**Primary Care — Immunization Counseling Codes:**
Two new codes cover immunization counseling when immunization is NOT administered by the provider on the same date:
- 90482: 3 minutes up to 10 minutes
- 90483: Greater than 10 minutes up to 20 minutes

**Cardiology — Cardiac Imaging and Echocardiography:**
New codes for advanced cardiac imaging techniques and updates to stress testing codes reflect current clinical protocols. Echocardiography codes are being restructured to better differentiate imaging approaches.

**Orthopedics — Bundling Rule Refinements:**
Changes to joint injection, arthroscopy, and fracture care codes are accompanied by refined bundling rules that affect what can be billed separately on the same surgical encounter.

**HCPCS and NCCI:** HCPCS Level II codes and NCCI edits are updated quarterly; practices should check Q2 2026 updates (effective April 1) for any edits affecting their high-volume code pairs.

**Warning:** Practices still using deleted codes or pre-2026 crosswalks risk systematic claim rejections. Conduct a focused audit of your top 50 procedure codes against the 2026 CPT changes before April 1.

High-performing practices are operating with a new set of RCM metrics in 2026. The concept of "Zero-Day Denials" — where claims are corrected and resubmitted within 24 hours of a rejection — has moved from aspirational to operational reality at practices deploying AI-enabled denial management tools. The traditional 30-day A/R target is now a baseline; industry leaders are pushing toward 25 days.

| Days in A/R (Net) | 35–45 days | Under 30 days | 25 days |
| Gross Days in A/R | 55–75 days | Under 50 days | 40 days |

**Front-End Accuracy Is the Fastest Lever:**
Errors at registration are the leading cause of delayed or denied claims. Front-end automation — real-time eligibility verification at every visit, digital intake forms, and EHR-to-billing sync — directly reduces A/R days more efficiently than back-end recovery.

**Automation Compounds Returns:**
- Automated claims processing improves first-pass claim rates by 25% and reduces denial rates by up to 30%
- Modern denial management workflows that immediately flag denials, prioritize high-value claims, and track appeal deadlines improve recovery rates by 45% and reduce A/R days by 22%

**The "Zero-Day Denial" Operational Model:**
Rather than batching denied claims for weekly review, leading practices now process denials in real-time with automated triage: AI categorizes the denial reason, identifies the fix, routes it to the correct team member, and tracks the resubmission deadline — all within hours of the original rejection.

**Action Required:** Run a quarterly revenue cycle audit targeting these four specific metrics: charge capture accuracy, denial patterns by payer and code, collection rate by financial class, and AR aging by bucket. Document baseline, target, and accountable owner for each metric.

## Section 07 — Compliance Corner
### Price Transparency Enforcement Clock Starts April 1 — Machine-Readable File Requirements Get Stricter

CMS finalized significant updates to hospital price transparency regulations effective January 1, 2026, with enforcement beginning April 1, 2026. The three-month enforcement delay was granted to give hospitals time to update their systems, validate data, and post compliant files. That window closes at the end of this month.

**Key New Requirements (Effective January 1, 2026, Enforced April 1, 2026):**

**Machine-Readable File (MRF) Changes:**
- Hospitals must now publish **median allowed amounts** (replacing the estimated allowed amount) in their MRF when payer-specific negotiated charges are expressed as percentages or algorithms
- Must also publish **10th percentile and 90th percentile allowed amounts** alongside the median
- MRFs must include **Type 2 National Provider Identifiers** (NPI-2 codes associated with primary tax codes indicating hospital unit or hospital) — employer identification numbers (EINs) are no longer acceptable identifiers

**Enforcement and Penalty Modifications:**
- Starting January 1, 2026, hospitals that violate price transparency requirements can reduce their penalty by 35% by waiving their right to a hearing before an administrative law judge
- CMS conducted a webinar February 11, 2026, specifically addressing the updated requirements; recording is available

**Interoperability and FHIR:**
The CMS Interoperability and Prior Authorization Final Rule (CMS-0057-F) continues implementation, requiring payers to support FHIR-based APIs for patient data access, provider directory queries, and prior authorization workflows. Practices should confirm that their PM/EHR vendors have integrated FHIR-compliant PA APIs before the 2027 real-time mandate.

**Warning:** Ambulatory surgical centers (ASCs) face parallel price transparency requirements under the CY 2026 OPPS/ASC Final Rule. Independent practices that own or operate ASC facilities should audit compliance immediately — the April 1 enforcement start is this fiscal quarter.

Independent and small-group physician practices are entering Q2 2026 under intensifying financial pressure. A convergence of payer network pullbacks, rising technology costs, workforce instability, and growing denial rates is narrowing the margin between a practice that stays independent and one that is forced to sell or merge. MGMA data shows 37% of practice spending is going toward workforce stabilization and another 30% toward health IT and cybersecurity — leaving less flexibility for revenue cycle investment precisely when it is most needed.

**Financial Pressure Indicators:**
- 62% of RCM leaders cite denials and underpayment management as their top obstacle in 2026
- 46% cite reimbursement pressure as a primary concern
- 44% name staffing constraints as a major challenge
- More than one-third of providers report denial rates of 10% or more — double the recommended benchmark of under 5%
- Industry RCM cost benchmark: ~5% of collections, but many independent practices run at 8–12%

### Your Priority RCM Checklist for the Week of March 20, 2026
