## Section 01 &mdash; Regulatory / Payment Updates

### OBBBA Goes Live: Montana and Arkansas Activate Medicaid Work Requirements July 1 &mdash; The First-State Playbook Every Practice Needs Before January 2027

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) Medicaid work requirements have moved from policy to reality. Montana activated Medicaid community engagement requirements on **July 1, 2026**, and Arkansas launched a **soft implementation July 1** with penalties deferred to January 1, 2027. These are the first two states to cross the implementation threshold &mdash; and every state must follow by December 31, 2026 to comply with the law's mandate.

**What "Going Live" Means Operationally for Physician Practices:**

When a state activates work requirements, the following administrative chain begins for practices:

1. **Eligibility volatility spikes immediately.** Medicaid enrollees who fail to report 80 hours/month of community engagement are subject to suspension. Because documentation failures are common in the first months of any new requirement, practices in Montana and Arkansas should anticipate mid-month eligibility changes on patients they expected to be covered. Real-time eligibility (RTE) checks at the point of service &mdash; not just at scheduling &mdash; become mandatory for Medicaid patients.

2. **Medical frailty exemption certification demand emerges.** The AMA has issued detailed guidance (June 30, 2026) on the medical frailty exemption: physicians in Montana and Arkansas may now receive requests to certify that a patient has a qualifying medical condition, functional limitation, or caregiving need that exempts them from the 80-hour requirement. This documentation workflow is new and must be integrated into EHR note templates now.

3. **Claims mid-treatment disruption risk is real.** If a patient loses eligibility after an initial visit but before follow-up care, the claim for the second visit will be denied. The IFR (CMS-2454-IFC) provides limited retroactive reinstatement windows, making it critical to catch eligibility lapses before services are rendered.

**The National Timeline (As of July 3, 2026):**

- Montana: **Live July 1, 2026** (full implementation)
- Arkansas: **Soft launch July 1, 2026** (penalties begin January 1, 2027)
- All other states: Must notify enrollees by August 31, 2026 and implement by December 31, 2026
- Federal CMS review of state plans is ongoing; 14 additional states have submitted implementation plans

**CMS July 2026 Operational Updates (Effective July 6, 2026):**

Separately, CMS published its July 2026 quarterly operational updates via the 2026 Transmittals system, effective July 6, 2026:

- National Coverage Determination (NCD) updates
- Implementation of editing for PACE (Programs of All-Inclusive Care for the Elderly) inpatient claims for indirect medical education payment
- Records and information management requirements updates

**Stat:** **Two states** (Montana, Arkansas) now have OBBBA Medicaid work requirements active as of July 1, 2026 &mdash; the first in the nation. All remaining states face a December 31, 2026 implementation deadline. Practices with Medicaid-heavy panels in any state have approximately **six months** to build the eligibility monitoring and exemption documentation infrastructure required to prevent a wave of claim denials in Q1 2027.

## Section 02 &mdash; Prior Authorization / Payer Relations

### NewYork-Presbyterian Exits UHC Medicare Advantage Network July 1: The Cascade Effect on Independent Practices and What Your Eligibility Workflow Must Catch Now

The Medicare Advantage network contraction that defined the first half of 2026 added its most visible mid-year milestone: **NewYork-Presbyterian Hospitals and Medical Group practices became out-of-network for most UnitedHealthcare Medicare Advantage members on July 1, 2026.** Patients in active treatment before July 1 receive continuity-of-care protection through September 28, 2026 &mdash; but only if their practice identifies them and bills correctly.

**What Happened and Why It Matters:**

- NYP is one of the most-referred-to academic medical centers in the Northeast. Independent practices that refer NYP patients, receive referrals from NYP, or bill alongside NYP in shared care episodes face immediate disruption.
- The July 1 transition means patients who were in-network at their last visit may now be out-of-network &mdash; triggering higher cost-sharing and, critically, **prior authorization denials on follow-up claims that reference NYP's NPI as the servicing or ordering provider.**
- At least **23 major health systems** had exited Medicare Advantage networks by mid-year 2026, displacing approximately 2.9 million MA enrollees.

**The "Continuity-of-Care" Billing Risk:**

The September 28 continuity window is protected, but it requires active management:
- Claims for NYP out-of-network patients during this window must be billed correctly with the appropriate modifier or authorization number to receive in-network rates
- The window is patient-specific and service-specific &mdash; not a blanket extension
- Practices not actively tracking which patients are in the continuity window will generate CO-4 or CO-97 denials on those claims

**CMS-0057-F FHIR Prior Authorization API: January 2027 Deadline Approaches:**

With only six months until the CMS Interoperability and Prior Authorization Final Rule (CMS-0057-F) January 1, 2027 effective date, practices and their technology vendors should be actively testing:
- **Patient Access API**: Patient data portability
- **Provider Directory API**: Network accuracy
- **Prior Authorization API (FHIR-based)**: Real-time ePA submission and response
- **Payers covered**: Medicare Advantage, Medicaid MCOs, CHIP, QHP issuers on FFEs

Payers who are not compliant by January 1, 2027 face CMS enforcement action. For practices, the benefit is faster PA decisions &mdash; but only if they are connected to a clearinghouse or RCM system that has built the API integration.

**Stat:** **$6 billion** &mdash; annual administrative cost of prior authorization in U.S. physician practices (HFMA estimate). The combination of network exits (which trigger new PA requirements as patients move out-of-network) and the upcoming FHIR PA mandate means 2026 is the most consequential PA reform year in a decade. Practices not updating their PA workflows now will absorb both the cost and the delay.

## Section 03 &mdash; AI & Automation Deep Dive

### From Task-Level to System-Level: Why 63% AI Adoption With Only 15% Full Integration Is RCM's Most Dangerous Gap in 2026

A July 2, 2026 deep dive from Healthcare IT Today surfaced the defining tension in RCM AI adoption: **most AI in revenue cycle is operating at the task level, not the system level** &mdash; and that gap is the reason practices with AI tools are still seeing denial rates climb and AR days stay flat.

**The Task vs. System Level Distinction:**

Task-level AI handles one step in isolation: auto-verify eligibility, flag a coding error, draft an appeal letter. System-level AI connects across the workflow: it knows that the eligibility check at scheduling revealed a deductible not-yet-met, routes the claim through a pre-submission scrub that catches the documentation gap, and pre-empts the denial that would have been filed 45 days later.

According to industry analysis:
- **63%** of healthcare organizations have integrated AI-powered automation into revenue cycle workflows in some capacity
- Only **15%** have fully integrated AI into standard RCM operations
- **80%** of health systems are actively exploring, piloting, or implementing generative AI for RCM
- The top four AI applications with measurable returns: ambient documentation, CDI, autonomous coding, and electronic prior authorization (ePA)

**Case Study: OHSU + CodaMetrix &mdash; 70% Coding Denial Reduction**

Oregon Health & Science University Hospital (OHSU), working with CodaMetrix on radiology coding, achieved approximately **70% reduction in coding-related denials** on autonomously coded cases compared to manually coded cases. The result is significant because it validates a specific AI application (radiology autonomous coding) rather than generic productivity claims.

The mechanism: CodaMetrix's engine reads clinical documentation in real time, applies payer-specific rules at the code selection layer, and generates claims with the documentation specificity that prevents CO-4 (non-covered service) denials.

**The Integration Gap Is Where Revenue Is Lost:**

The 63%/15% gap is not a technology problem &mdash; it is a workflow architecture problem. Half of healthcare leaders cite data privacy and security concerns as the biggest barrier to AI adoption, and **41% say it's difficult to trust AI's results fully.** The organizations closing the gap are not deploying more tools &mdash; they are connecting the tools they have into end-to-end workflows with human oversight reserved for flagged exceptions.

McKinsey's 2026 survey of 215 revenue cycle leaders confirms the shift: **RCM is moving from a back-office function to a strategic organizational priority.** The leaders seeing the highest ROI are those who combined AI investment with workflow redesign, not just tool procurement.

**Stat:** AI coding engines achieve **99.2% accuracy** with clinical documentation processing &mdash; compared to an industry average human coder accuracy rate of approximately 81%. Practices using AI-powered RCM report an average first-pass claim rate of **98.7%** vs. 78&ndash;82% for manual workflows. The question is not whether AI works &mdash; it is whether your organization has built the system to deploy it at scale.

## Section 04 &mdash; Coding Updates

### NCCI Q3 2026 Live July 1 &mdash; HCPCS Quarterly Update Effective July 6: The Coding Changes Every Practice Must Audit Before Filing Q3 Claims

The July 1, 2026 NCCI Q3 Add-On Code (AOC) edit release and the July 6, 2026 HCPCS quarterly update are now live. These are the highest-risk coding transition windows of the year because they introduce simultaneous changes across multiple code families &mdash; and claims filed with pre-July 1 assumptions will generate NCCI bundling errors and MUE violations.

**NCCI Q3 2026 Add-On Code Edits (Effective July 1, 2026):**

CMS released Version Q3-2026 of the NCCI Add-On Code edit file. Key changes:

- All Practitioner Services PTP Quarterly Additions, Deletions, and Revisions are now effective
- NCCI Medically Unlikely Edits (MUE) updated: DME Supplier Services, Facility Outpatient Hospital Services, and Practitioner Services MUE tables all refreshed
- **ESRD Facility Change (Critical):** As of July 1, 2026, ESRD Facilities shall **no longer report the AX modifier** to receive TDAPA (Transitional Drug Add-on Payment Adjustment), TPNIES (Transitional Add-on Payment Adjustment for New and Innovative Equipment and Supplies), and CRA TPNIES payments. CMS will now automatically make these adjustments by combining the appropriate HCPCS codes with a valid revenue code. Practices billing ESRD codes with the AX modifier after July 1 will have claims processed incorrectly.

**HCPCS July 2026 Quarterly Update (Effective July 6, 2026):**

The July 2026 Alpha-Numeric HCPCS file was updated June 17, 2026 for implementation July 6, 2026. Key categories updated:
- New HCPCS Level II codes for DME and supplies
- Average Sales Price (ASP) Medicare Part B drug pricing files &mdash; updated quarterly; new pricing affects all Part B drug billing from July 6
- Claims Adjustment Reason Code (CARC) and Remittance Advice Remark Code (RARC) updates &mdash; EOB interpretation changes

**CPT 2026 AI Augmentation Codes &mdash; Q3 First Full Filing Quarter:**

CPT 2026 significantly expanded the Assistive and Augmentative AI Services section. Q3 2026 is the first full quarter where practices should be billing the new codes:
- **New code for algorithmic analysis of encephalographic (EEG) waveforms** &mdash; AI-assisted EEG interpretation
- **New code for connectomic analysis of the brain** &mdash; for neurology practices using advanced neuroimaging analytics
- **New RPM codes 99445 and 99470** &mdash; remote physiologic monitoring treatment management services after 10 minutes of service per calendar month (also applicable to primary care)

**ICD-10-CM/PCS FY 2027 Preview (Effective October 1, 2026):**

CMS has already released the FY 2027 ICD-10-CM and ICD-10-PCS code sets. ICD-10-CM diagnosis code updates apply to patient encounters from **October 1, 2026 through September 30, 2027**. Practices should begin encoder updates and coder training in July and August to avoid October 1 claim errors.

**Stat:** CMS posts NCCI PTP edit changes on a quarterly basis; the Q3 2026 release includes changes across all three MUE tables (DME, Facility Outpatient, Practitioner). Practices with high ESRD billing volume must update their billing systems to remove AX modifier from TDAPA claims **by July 6, 2026** or they will generate incorrectly processed payments that require adjustment.

## Section 05 &mdash; Revenue Velocity / KPIs

### 2026 RCM Benchmark Deep Dive: Days in A/R Under 30, Clean Claims Above 98%, Denial Rate Below 5% &mdash; Where the Gap Is Hiding

McKinsey's 2026 survey of **215 revenue cycle leaders** found that RCM has officially moved from a back-office cost center to a **board-level strategic priority.** The shift brings new pressure on KPI performance &mdash; and updated benchmarks that expose how wide the performance gap has grown between top-performing AI-native practices and the median.

**2026 RCM Benchmark Table:**

| Metric | Industry Median | Top Performer Target | AI-Native Practices | Warning Threshold |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Days in A/R | 40&ndash;45 days | Under 30 days | Under 25 days | Over 50 days |
| Clean Claims Rate (1st pass) | 91&ndash;93% | 95%+ | 98.7% | Below 90% |
| Denial Rate | 8&ndash;12% | Below 5% | Below 3% | Above 15% |
| Net Collection Rate | 91&ndash;93% | 98%+ | 99%+ | Below 87% |
| A/R >90 Days (% of total) | 18&ndash;22% | Below 10% | Below 8% | Above 25% |

**The Gap Is Not Evenly Distributed:**

Initial denial rates across the industry reached **11.8% in 2024** and are projected to climb into 2026 as payer audit intensity increases post-OBBBA. Hospitals spent **nearly $18 billion overturning denials in 2025** &mdash; a cost that is itself a benchmark failure signal. When practices are spending that much on appeals, it means front-end prevention (eligibility, coding accuracy, pre-authorization) is failing.

**The Three Hidden Gaps Most Practices Miss:**

1. **A/R Aging Distribution:** Most practices track average A/R days but miss the 90+ day bucket. A practice with 38-day average A/R can still have 30% of its A/R over 90 days if collections are not actively worked. The best indicator is the percentage of A/R over 90 days, not average days.

2. **Specialty-Specific vs. Blended Benchmarks:** The HFMA MAP Keys benchmark sets physician practice median Days in A/R at 21.9 days &mdash; far below the 40-day hospital benchmark. Blending specialty and facility benchmarks produces misleading targets.

3. **First-Pass Rate vs. Acceptance Rate:** Clearinghouse acceptance rate (whether a claim was accepted for processing) is not the same as first-pass payment rate (whether the claim was paid on first submission). Tracking clearinghouse acceptance as the clean claims proxy overstates performance by 10&ndash;15 percentage points.

**Action Insight:** Practices seeing AR days over 45 or denial rates above 10% should run a root-cause denial category analysis before the end of Q3. The top three denial categories in 2026 are: (1) eligibility/coverage not in effect, (2) missing or invalid authorization, and (3) coding/bundling errors. Each maps directly to a preventable front-end workflow failure.

**Stat:** The HFMA MAP Keys benchmark sets **top-performing physician practices at 21.9 Days in A/R** &mdash; meaning high-performing independent practices are collecting nearly twice as fast as the industry median of 40&ndash;45 days. The performance gap is not primarily a payer problem &mdash; it is a front-end and coding workflow problem that AI-powered automation can close within 60&ndash;90 days of deployment.

## Section 07 &mdash; Compliance Corner

### Q4 2026 Compliance Calendar: CMS-0062-P Final Rule Imminent, OBBBA State Outreach Deadline August 31, ICD-10 FY 2027 Codes Active October 1

The second half of 2026 brings three major compliance convergences. Practices that are not actively tracking all three will be reacting to each one individually rather than preparing for the compound impact.

**Event 1: CMS-0062-P Drug Prior Authorization Final Rule &mdash; Expected Q4 2026**

The comment period for CMS's 2026 Drug Prior Authorization Interoperability Proposed Rule (CMS-0062-P) closed June 15, 2026. CMS is expected to publish the final rule in **Q4 2026 (November&ndash;December)**. The rule, once finalized, will:
- Require electronic PA for all drug-related coverage requests (medical and pharmacy benefit)
- Set decision timelines: urgent requests 24 hours; standard requests 72 hours for MA/Medicaid
- Mandate FHIR-based (Prior Authorization API) and NCPDP SCRIPT/F&B/RTPB-standard electronic submission by **October 1, 2027**
- Apply to all Medicare Advantage, Medicaid MCOs, CHIP, and QHP issuers on the FFE

Practices should use Q4 2026 to confirm their clearinghouse and EHR/PM vendor has a CMS-0062-P readiness roadmap.

**Event 2: OBBBA State Outreach Deadline &mdash; August 31, 2026**

All states must complete outreach to Medicaid enrollees about upcoming work requirements **by August 31, 2026**. When enrollees receive notice, they often turn to their physician's office with questions. Practices should:
- Train front desk and care coordination staff on what work requirements mean
- Have patient education materials available in multiple languages
- Document any conversation in which a patient discloses a medical condition that may qualify for exemption

**Event 3: ICD-10-CM/PCS FY 2027 Codes &mdash; October 1, 2026**

ICD-10-CM diagnosis codes and ICD-10-PCS procedure codes for FY 2027 become effective **October 1, 2026**. Based on the FY 2026 update precedent, practices should expect:
- New codes for emerging diagnoses (AI-flagged conditions, behavioral health expansions)
- Revised guidelines for BMI, HIV, and Type 2 diabetes coding (FY 2026 changes may have downstream FY 2027 revisions)
- New codes in E&M documentation for telehealth modalities
- Encoder and EHR updates must be complete by **September 30, 2026**

**Price Transparency Enforcement Update:**

CMS continues enforcement of hospital price transparency requirements, with civil monetary penalty authority now extended to outpatient settings in some states. While physician practices are not yet subject to the same requirements as hospitals, the regulatory direction is clear: payer-specific fee schedules and out-of-pocket estimates will eventually be required at the physician practice level.

**Stat:** **October 1, 2027** &mdash; the mandatory implementation date for FHIR-based electronic prior authorization under CMS-0062-P (if finalized in Q4 2026 as expected). With 15 months of lead time, practices have exactly one annual budget cycle to evaluate and select a FHIR-compliant PA solution. Do not wait for the final rule to begin vendor evaluation.

### Independent Practice Watch: OBBBA Work Requirements Hit Ground Zero in Two States &mdash; Your Medicaid Panel Risk Model Must Be Done Before August

For independent practices, the OBBBA work requirements are not a future risk to monitor &mdash; they are a **present operational challenge** as of July 1, 2026. Montana and Arkansas practices are the first to face the new eligibility volatility, but the infrastructure required to manage it is the same infrastructure every practice in every state must build by January 2027.

**What Independent Practices Face Differently Than Health Systems:**

Health systems have eligibility verification at scale as a managed service, often with automated real-time RTE running 24/7 across their PM systems. Independent practices &mdash; particularly solo and small-group practices &mdash; typically verify eligibility at scheduling and again at check-in, with a 24-48 hour delay built in. **That gap is now a revenue risk.**

When a Medicaid patient's eligibility changes mid-month due to a work requirement reporting failure, the practice that verified on Monday may serve that patient on Thursday with a claim that will be denied. Without continuous or daily eligibility monitoring, the practice will not discover the lapse until the EOB arrives 30&ndash;60 days later.

**Where Independent Practices Should Be: Medicaid Panel Risk Benchmarks**

| Area | Minimum Viable | Best Practice | Where Most Independent Practices Are |
|---|---|---|---|
| Eligibility Check Frequency (Medicaid) | At scheduling + day of service | Daily batch for all active Medicaid patients | At scheduling only |
| OBBBA Frailty Exemption Protocol | Template in EHR | Proactive documentation for qualifying patients | No protocol established |
| Medicaid Revenue Attrition Modeling | Annual | Quarterly, by panel concentration | Not done |
| Staff Training on Work Requirements | Front desk briefing | Full team + patient education materials | None |

**The Revenue Attrition Model: How to Build It in 30 Days**

For practices with more than 15% Medicaid panel concentration, a simple attrition model should be built before the August 31 enrollee outreach deadline:

1. Pull Medicaid patient census and segment by age (19&ndash;64, the work-requirement-eligible range)
2. Apply a 5&ndash;15% attrition assumption over 12 months (conservative to moderate based on CBO projections)
3. Calculate revenue impact: (Medicaid revenue &times; Medicaid %) &times; attrition rate = projected revenue at risk
4. Compare to cost of enhanced eligibility monitoring tools (&lt;$500/month for most PM-integrated solutions)
5. The ROI of monitoring infrastructure is immediate at any practice with &gt;10% Medicaid concentration

**Stat:** The AMA estimates **10&ndash;17 million** Medicaid enrollees will lose coverage by 2034 under OBBBA work requirements. For an independent primary care practice with 200 active Medicaid patients, even a 5% attrition rate means 10 patients &mdash; and 10 patients worth of claim denials to catch, appeal, or absorb. The cost of daily eligibility monitoring ($500/month = $6,000/year) is recovered by preventing three claim denials.

### Action Items for the Week of July 3, 2026

1. **Remove AX Modifier from All ESRD TDAPA Claims Immediately.** As of July 1, 2026, the AX modifier is no longer required (and will cause processing errors) for ESRD TDAPA, TPNIES, and CRA TPNIES payments. CMS now automatically applies these adjustments by combining the appropriate HCPCS codes with valid revenue codes. Update your billing system configuration and payer edit rules today. Any ESRD claims submitted with AX after July 1 will need adjustment.

OBBBA work requirements went live July 1 in both states; eligibility can now change mid-month. Every other state has until December 31 to implement &mdash; begin your eligibility monitoring upgrade now.

3. **Confirm Your Vendor's CMS-0057-F FHIR PA API Readiness.** The CMS Interoperability and Prior Authorization Final Rule (CMS-0057-F) requires FHIR-based Prior Authorization API compliance by January 1, 2027. Ask your clearinghouse and EHR/PM vendor for their readiness timeline. If they cannot confirm a Q3 or Q4 2026 launch date, begin evaluating alternatives now.

4. **Run the Three-Part RCM Performance Gap Audit.** Pull your Q2 2026 data for: (a) Days in A/R; (b) first-pass clean claims rate (from clearinghouse to payment, not just clearinghouse acceptance); (c) denial root-cause breakdown by denial category code.
