## Section 01 — Regulatory / Payment Updates

### OBBBA's Coverage Unraveling: 10–17M Medicaid Patients at Risk, $24B in Uncompensated Care Projected for Physicians — Revenue Attrition Modeling Is Now an RCM Priority

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed July 4, 2025, is entering its most consequential implementation phase. While the June 3, 2026 CMS Interim Final Rule (CMS-2454-IFC) established the federal framework for Medicaid work reporting requirements, a critical analysis published June 24, 2026 by health law firm Mintz details what these requirements mean operationally for physician practices — and the revenue implications are material enough to require immediate RCM action.

**What the OBBBA Does to Health Coverage:**
- **Medicaid**: Work/community engagement requirements (80 hours/month) active by January 1, 2027 in most states. Enrollment reviews tightened. Estimates range from 10 million to 17 million enrollees losing coverage by 2034.
- **ACA Marketplace**: Enhanced premium tax credits (EPTCs), the subsidies that made marketplace plans affordable for 15+ million Americans, expired at end of 2025. Millions of marketplace enrollees are now facing unaffordable premiums in 2026.
- **Provider Taxes**: OBBBA permanently restricts states from creating new provider taxes or increasing rates on existing ones — cutting a primary funding lever states use to draw down federal Medicaid matching funds.

**Revenue Impact for Physician Practices:**
- Estimated **$24 billion** increase in uncompensated care for physicians over 10 years
- Practices with Medicaid concentration above 20% face meaningful volume and payer-mix disruption
- Emergency physicians, OB/GYNs, and primary care physicians face disproportionate uncompensated care burden under EMTALA
- The ACA EPTC expiration compounds Medicaid losses: practices with dual Medicaid/ACA patient panels face double exposure

**New Provider Obligations Under CMS-2454-IFC:**
As detailed in the June 24 Mintz analysis, physician practices face new administrative duties under the work requirement framework:
1. **Medical exemption certification**: Physicians may be asked to certify that a patient has a medical condition, functional limitation, or caregiving need that qualifies them for exemption from work requirements — creating documentation demand that mirrors disability certification workflows
2. **Patient education**: Practices may need to educate patients on Medicaid work-reporting obligations and renewal processes to prevent surprise coverage lapses that generate claim denials
3. **Claims disruption risk**: When patients lose Medicaid eligibility due to administrative failures, claims submitted mid-treatment will be denied with limited retroactive reinstatement options

**The Medicare Payment Parallel:**
The AMA's June 18, 2026 National Advocacy Update highlights an intersecting Medicare payment crisis: physician payments have declined **33% in real terms since 2001** when adjusted for practice-cost inflation. The OBBBA's temporary 2.5% conversion factor update for 2026 provides a one-year lifeline but does not address the structural gap. The Strengthening Medicare for Patients and Providers Act (H.R. 6160) — which would permanently tie Medicare payments to the Medical Economic Index — has bipartisan support but no scheduled floor vote.

**Stat:** **10–17 million** — estimated Medicaid enrollees projected to lose coverage under OBBBA's work requirements and eligibility tightening. For a 5-physician primary care group with 25% Medicaid concentration, this equates to modeling 5–15% patient attrition over the next 3–5 years — a number large enough to require a formal revenue attrition model before year-end.

### Cigna's 70% PA Standardization by Year-End: The Full Payer PA Scorecard After the June 15 Drug PA Comment Deadline

In a payer landscape still defined by inconsistency, Cigna's April 24, 2026 commitment to standardize prior authorization submission requirements for 70%+ of its PA volume by year-end 2026 represents the most comprehensive structural PA reform by a commercial insurer. The commitment joins an accelerating multi-payer wave reshaping the PA administrative burden on physician practices.

**The Cigna PA Standardization (April 24, 2026):**
- By December 31, 2026: A single standardized approach will apply to medical services representing **more than 70% of Cigna's prior authorization volume**
- Services targeted: Orthopedic surgeries, CT scans, MRIs, and other commonly reviewed imaging and procedural services
- Cigna has already **reduced overall PA volume by 15%** since early 2025
- The standard is designed to enable real-time electronic approvals — not just eliminating PAs but standardizing the remaining ones into predictable, processable workflows
- Additional services will be added on a rolling basis beyond year-end 2026

**2026 Payer PA Scorecard (As of June 26, 2026):**

|---|---|---|
| UnitedHealthcare | 30% of services eliminated by year-end | Echocardiograms, outpatient surgery, therapies; replacing with AI-powered post-payment audit |

| Industry Coalition | 11% overall reduction since June 2025 | 50 insurers, 257 million members |

**CMS-0062-P Drug PA Rule: Comment Period Now Closed**

The June 15 comment deadline for CMS's 2026 Drug Prior Authorization Interoperability rule (CMS-0062-P) has now passed. Key facts:
- If finalized, would require electronic PA for all drug-related coverage requests (medical and pharmacy benefit)
- Decision timelines: urgent requests 24 hours; standard requests 72 hours for MA/Medicaid
- Mandatory FHIR-based electronic submission by October 1, 2027
- CMS finalization expected Q4 2026 — watch for the final rule announcement in November–December

**What the PA Standardization Means for Practice Workflows:**

The Cigna standardization is the first commercial payer commitment to standardize (not just reduce) the PA process at scale. This distinction matters: when PA requirements are standardized, practices can build repeatable workflows rather than custom protocols for each payer. For practices submitting 50+ PAs per week, a standardized submission format can reduce per-PA handling time by 30–40% — and enables automation tools that depend on consistency to actually work.

**Stat:** **70%+** — the share of Cigna's prior authorization volume that will be covered by a single standardized submission standard by year-end 2026. Combined with UHC's 30% elimination, the PA landscape is restructuring faster than most practices have updated their workflows to reflect. Practices still using payer-specific custom PA processes for Cigna are overcomplicating workflows that will be standardized by December.

**UiPath's Full-Stack Healthcare RCM Framework (June 2026):**

UiPath's Business Orchestration and Automation Technology (BOAT) architecture represents the field's most mature attempt to solve the fragmentation problem through three integrated layers:

1. **Medical Records Summarization**: Converts multi-thousand-page patient records into structured, cite-traceable summaries at the point of prior authorization, clinical documentation review, and appeals — eliminating the 45–90 minutes per complex case that staff spend searching for supporting documentation
2. **Claim Denial Prevention and Resolution**: AI automatically detects denial root causes, triggers corrective actions, and orchestrates appeal workflows — connecting claim-level detection directly to revenue recovery without manual handoffs between systems
3. **Prior Authorization Automation**: End-to-end PA workflow from eligibility verification through medical necessity mapping, intelligent routing, and real-time status tracking — reducing PA cycle time from days to hours

- **25% increase** in clinical-ground denials in 2025 vs. 2024 (Kodiak Solutions) — payers are using AI to deny faster than most providers are using AI to prevent

**The Denial Surge That Full-Stack Orchestration Is Designed to Address:**

Kodiak Solutions' 2026 research finding is the starkest data point in this week's AI landscape: insurers denied **more claims on clinical grounds in 2025 than in 2024**, increasing net revenue leakage at hospitals by 25%. This is the specific problem that full-stack orchestration — not another point tool — addresses: payer AI is outpacing provider AI unless the provider's tools are connected across the full claim lifecycle. Practices with five separate AI tools operating in silos are generating five separate data trails that never converge into a claim-level intelligence picture.

**Stat:** **86%** — share of healthcare leaders who report leaving revenue on the table due to fragmentation between point-solution AI tools, even as 100% express interest in a unified AI-powered platform connecting mid-cycle workflows to final claim submission and collections (Waystar, June 2026). The AI investment is being made; the ROI is being lost between the tools.

## Section 04 — Coding Updates

### Neurology and Radiology Coding Alert: EEG Code Family Revisions, Six New MRI-Safety CPT Codes (76014–76019), and Expanding PA Requirements

With the October 1, 2026 FY 2027 ICD-10 changes already on the countdown calendar, two specialty coding areas are generating urgent near-term denial risk: neurology's EEG and nerve conduction code family revisions effective January 2026, and radiology's six new MRI-safety CPT codes (76014–76019) that most practices have not yet integrated into charge masters.

**Neurology 2026 Coding Changes (Effective January 1, 2026):**

The AMA's CPT 2026 revisions significantly reorganized the electroencephalography (EEG) code family and revised nerve conduction study codes:
- **EEG code family**: Revised CPT descriptions for long-term video-EEG monitoring — practices billing the pre-2026 code structure for continuous inpatient monitoring are generating NCCI bundling issues; Q1–Q2 2026 EEG claims should be audited immediately
- **Nerve conduction studies**: Updated code descriptions now require specific documentation of the number of nerve segments studied — claims without this specificity are generating CO-4 (non-covered service) denials
- **PA expansion**: Commercial payers (UHC, Aetna) have expanded prior authorization requirements for long-term video-EEG monitoring and tightened medical necessity criteria for repeat nerve conduction studies — affecting practices that historically submitted without PA

**Radiology: Six New MRI-Safety CPT Codes (76014–76019):**

Six new Category I CPT codes were introduced effective January 1, 2026 to address the growing clinical need to formally assess implanted devices before performing MRI:

| Code | Service Described |
|---|---|
| 76014 | MR conditional evaluation — cardiac devices |
| 76015 | MR conditional evaluation — cochlear implants |
| 76016 | MR conditional evaluation — neurostimulators |
| 76017 | MR conditional evaluation — orthopedic hardware |
| 76018 | MR conditional evaluation — other metallic implants |
| 76019 | MR conditional evaluation — complex/multiple device assessment |

These codes apply when a patient has implanted devices requiring formal MR conditional analysis before imaging. This service was previously billed inconsistently under unlisted or evaluation codes. Practices that have not added these to their charge master are either not billing the service at all (revenue leakage) or billing under unlisted codes (generating claim scrutiny and denial risk).

**Audio-Only Telehealth Coding Alert:**

Cigna and Aetna have restricted audio-only telehealth codes (CPT 99441–99443) in 2026 as video capability has become more widely available. UHC and Humana have limited audio-only reimbursement to behavioral health and specific rural settings. Practices that maintain significant telephone-only patient populations — particularly behavioral health and primary care — should verify by payer which codes remain reimbursable before continuing to submit them.

**Stat:** **Six** new MRI-safety evaluation CPT codes (76014–76019) effective January 2026. Most have not yet been integrated into practice charge masters — creating a revenue capture gap for every radiology, neurology, and orthopedic practice that evaluates patients with implanted devices before MRI. If your practice performs pre-MRI device assessments and is not billing these codes, that revenue is leaving with every patient.

## Section 05 — Revenue Velocity / KPIs

### The Net Collection Rate Gap: 95%+ vs. 83–89% National Median — and the Front-End Fix That Closes It in 90 Days

Revenue cycle performance tracking in 2026 is evolving past denial rates and Days in AR toward the metric that most directly predicts long-run revenue capture: **Net Collection Rate (NCR)**. Best-in-class practices are clearing **93–95%+** NCR while the national median hovers at **83–89%** — a gap that translates to $150,000–$400,000 per physician per year in avoidable revenue loss.

**Why NCR Is the Right Tracking Metric:**
- Denial rate tells you how many claims fail first pass; NCR tells you how much of every dollar you are legally owed you actually collect
- Practices focused exclusively on denial rates often miss systematic write-offs — contractual adjustments used as a write-off proxy, uncollected patient balances, and underpayment settlements that never generate a "denial" but do reduce NCR
- The national median of 83–89% means the average practice is writing off 11–17 cents on every collectible dollar — a figure most practices have not explicitly calculated

**The Front-End Fix That Drives 90-Day NCR Improvement:**

Front-end verification — real-time insurance eligibility, authorization confirmation, and demographic accuracy at every encounter — catches **40–50% of all initial claim rejections** before they become denials. The systematic impact:
- Practices implementing comprehensive front-end verification protocols see a **3–5 day reduction in Days in AR** within 60 days
- Every 1% improvement in Clean Claims Rate (CCR) reduces Days in AR by **0.5–1.0 days** — a jump from 90% to 95% CCR is worth 2.5–5 AR days
- Organizations following structured 90-day improvement plans reduce Days in AR by **10–25%** — the kind of improvement that pays for a new billing system in year one

**2026 Revenue Velocity KPI Benchmarks:**

| KPI | Danger Zone | Median | Target | Best in Class |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Net Collection Rate | < 83% | 83–89% | >= 93% | 95%+ |
| Clean Claims Rate | < 90% | 90–93% | >= 95% | 97–99% |
| Days in AR | > 50 | 40–49 | 30–40 | < 25 |
| First-Pass Acceptance | < 85% | 87–91% | >= 95% | 97%+ |
| Denial Rate | > 15% | 10–12% | < 8% | < 5.7% |
| AR Over 90 Days | > 20% | 15–20% | < 12% | < 8% |

**The Clinical Denial Surge (Kodiak Solutions 2026):**

Even as administrative denials are being addressed by PA reform and eligibility automation, clinical-ground denials surged **25% in 2025** compared to 2024. Payers are deploying AI to deny clinical medical necessity on more claims, faster. Practices that address front-end administrative performance but ignore clinical documentation improvement (CDI) will hit a ceiling: their denial rate improves for administrative reasons but degrades for clinical ones. The two-track problem requires a two-track solution.

**Stat:** The gap between best-in-class NCR (95%+) and the national median (83–89%) translates to **$150,000–$400,000 per physician annually** in avoidable revenue loss — larger than most practices realize because it never appears as a denied claim. It shows up as write-offs, underpayments, and aging AR that is eventually contractually adjusted rather than collected.

## Section 07 — Compliance Corner

### OBBBA Provider Tax Freeze, Audio-Only Telehealth Restrictions, and the H2 2026 Compliance Calendar

Three compliance issues are converging in Q3 2026 that don't fit neatly into single sections but collectively require immediate action from every practice before year-end.

**OBBBA Provider Tax Freeze — State Medicaid Funding at Risk:**

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act permanently restricts states from creating new provider taxes or increasing rates on existing ones — cutting the primary mechanism states use to generate state match for federal Medicaid funding. With this mechanism frozen:
- States with limited alternative funding sources may reduce Medicaid managed care organization payment rates to providers in 2027
- The CBO estimates $344 billion in reduced federal Medicaid outlays from FY2025–FY2034 — driven by enrollment reductions AND reduced state match capacity
- Practices in California, New York, Texas, and Florida — states that relied heavily on provider taxes — should monitor their state's Medicaid financing strategy through the second half of 2026

**Audio-Only Telehealth: Commercial Payer Restrictions Now Active:**

The 2026 commercial payer consensus is that telephone-only encounters (CPT 99441–99443) are no longer broadly reimbursable:
- **Cigna and Aetna**: Restricted audio-only codes to specific situations where video is not technically feasible
- **UHC and Humana**: Limited audio-only reimbursement to behavioral health and specific rural settings
- **Medicare**: Permanent telehealth extensions from CY 2026 PFS cover video-enabled telehealth; audio-only exception requires documentation that video was not possible
- Practices billing audio-only at 2024 volumes without payer verification are accumulating denial exposure in Q2–Q3 2026

**Medicare Mental Health Telehealth: In-Person First Visit Now Required:**

Under 2026 CMS guidelines, Medicare mental health patients must have an in-person visit before ongoing telehealth for mental health services can be billed — with exceptions for rural/HPSA settings. Practices that established all-telehealth mental health relationships during COVID-era flexibilities face claim rejection for those patients' ongoing telehealth mental health services if the baseline in-person visit is not documented.

### Independent Practice Watch: OBBBA Creates a Double Exposure — Medicaid and ACA Marketplace Panels at Risk Simultaneously; Your Revenue Attrition Model

The combination of OBBBA Medicaid work requirements (January 2027) and ACA enhanced premium tax credit expiration (end of 2025) is creating a compounding coverage crisis that hits independent practices hardest. Health systems absorb coverage disruption across large patient volumes; a 3–5-physician group that loses 15–20% of its Medicaid and ACA patients simultaneously cannot absorb the fixed-cost pressure.

**The Double Exposure Math:**
- A 3-physician primary care group with 25% Medicaid and 12% ACA marketplace patients has **37% of its payer mix** at elevated risk from OBBBA/EPTC changes
- If 20% of Medicaid patients lose coverage and 15% of ACA patients become uninsured, the practice loses **7–9% of total revenue** — before accounting for increased bad debt from remaining enrolled patients with reduced benefits
- This math worsens if the practice also has significant Medicare Advantage exposure following the 2026 MA network instability

**Certification Burden Is Coming:**

The June 24 Mintz analysis makes clear that physician practices will bear a new administrative duty: certifying medical exemptions for Medicaid patients who cannot meet work requirements. The workflow is specific:
- A patient with a chronic condition, caregiving obligation, or functional limitation can be exempted if a physician certifies the condition
- The certification must link the condition to a functional limitation on the ability to engage in work or community activities — general "patient has diabetes" is insufficient; the connection to functional limitation must be explicit
- Practices managing large panels of complex patients (primary care, behavioral health, physical therapy) need a standardized exemption documentation template and a staff workflow to process requests efficiently

## Section 09 — Specialty RCM Spotlight

### Specialty RCM Spotlight: Primary Care, Cardiology, Orthopedics, Oncology, Neurology, Radiology, Mental Health

**Primary Care:** CMS finalized a meaningful boost for independent primary care in the CY 2026 Medicare Physician Fee Schedule — E/M code reimbursement increases and expanded Principal Illness Navigation (PIN) codes (G0023, G0024, G0140, G0146) provide partial offset to conversion factor erosion. However, primary care faces the highest OBBBA double exposure: typically carrying 25–40% Medicaid concentration AND the highest ACA marketplace patient share. With ACA EPTCs already expired and Medicaid work requirements activating January 2027, primary care is the specialty with the most immediate revenue attrition risk. Stat: Primary care practices need an OBBBA attrition model in hand before August to make 2027 budget decisions.

**Cardiology:** Cardiology is absorbing a **$700 million cumulative Medicare reimbursement reduction in 2026** from the CMS-1832-F Physician Fee Schedule's -2.5% efficiency adjustment on procedure-heavy specialties. Practices attempting to recode in response without specialty-specific expertise are averaging 18–22% denial rate spikes in the first 90 days. The UHC PA elimination for echocardiograms (rolling out through December 2026) provides administrative relief but does not offset the reimbursement cut. The combination of procedure volume, coding complexity, and high Medicare Advantage concentration makes cardiology the clearest case for specialty-specific RCM optimization.

**Orthopedics:** The FY 2027 ICD-10-PCS spinal fusion device code changes (tables 0RG and 0SG, effective October 1) require charge master updates within the next 13 weeks. Orthopedic practices that have not started the update process should escalate now — custom-made interbody fusion device billing will generate systematic denials on October 1 without the update. The Cigna 70% PA standardization announcement is particularly relevant for orthopedics: CT scans and MRIs are primary targets of the standardization, and orthopedic PA workflows for these are among the most complex. Build the new standard Cigna PA workflows before year-end.

**Oncology:** OBBBA Medicaid and ACA marketplace coverage losses create a specific clinical risk for oncology: patients who lose coverage mid-treatment may need to interrupt chemotherapy, radiation, or biologic drug therapy. Practices should work with financial counselors to identify current Medicaid/ACA oncology patients and proactively assess 2027 coverage risk before December 2026. FY 2027 ICD-10-CM expanded metastatic cancer codes (C78.– and C79.– series) also require oncology documentation updates by October 1 — site-specific detail is required for medical necessity under most commercial and MA payer policies.

**Neurology:** The 2026 EEG code family revisions (effective January 2026) are generating systematic denials at neurology practices that have not updated charge masters or documentation templates. NCCI bundling edits for long-term video-EEG monitoring are being applied more aggressively. Commercial PA has also expanded for repeat nerve conduction studies. Neurology practices should immediately run a 90-day claims audit (January–March 2026) to identify EEG and nerve conduction study coding errors before they age out of timely appeal windows. This is an urgent, time-sensitive action.

**Radiology:** The six new MRI-safety evaluation CPT codes (76014–76019) effective January 2026 represent a revenue capture opportunity that most practices are missing entirely. These codes are not yet in most charge masters — and the service they describe (formally evaluating implanted devices before MRI) is being performed but not billed. Radiology also carries the highest PA burden of any specialty: 90%+ of commercial payers require authorization for CT, MRI, PET, and nuclear medicine. The ongoing UHC PA elimination and Cigna standardization will reduce this burden — but not immediately. Verify PA requirements by service type and payer before assuming they are eliminated.

**Mental Health:** The intersection of audio-only telehealth restrictions (Cigna/Aetna) and the Medicare in-person first-visit requirement for mental health services is creating a structural billing shift in 2026. Practices that built all-telehealth patient relationships during COVID-era flexibilities now face payer-by-payer reverification of which audio-only codes remain reimbursable. For Medicare patients, document the in-person baseline visit before continuing to bill ongoing telehealth for mental health. The good news: permanent Medicare telehealth extensions under CY 2026 PFS fully cover video-enabled mental health telehealth — the issue is audio-only, not telehealth broadly.
